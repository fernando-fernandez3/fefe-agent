## Goal Definition

A river retreat is the legacy place. Family memory-making, weekend getaways, time with the daughters in nature, an asset that holds value. The path is not "buy a property" — it is patient market scanning, capital readiness, decision-criteria refinement, and ultimately a specific transaction. The vision was revised from a working ranch (100–400 acres) to a river retreat (5–20 acres with frontage); the smaller footprint matches actual use (weekend getaways with young kids) and capital reality.

Constraints that should always travel with this goal:
- Texas Hill Country (within roughly 1–2 hours of San Antonio)
- Guadalupe River frontage preferred; comparable rivers (Comal, Frio, Medina) acceptable
- 5–20 acres
- $850k–$1.4M total budget
- No livestock requirement
- Airbnb income is optional, not required

## What the sources directly support

### 1. Land prices in the Hill Country are tracked, predictable in trend, volatile in specific tracts
**Source-backed point:** A&M's Texas Land Markets publishes per-acre price trends by region. Hill Country has been on a sustained appreciation curve.

**Goal implication:** "Wait for prices to drop" is not a strategy; the trend is the signal. Decision criteria should be price-per-acre relative to comparable recent transactions, not absolute dollar amount.

### 2. Water access and water rights are not the same thing
**Source-backed point:** Texas water law distinguishes surface water (state-controlled, with rights granted) from riparian access (which carries different rules). Frontage on a river ≠ unrestricted use.

**Goal implication:** Any property must be evaluated for its actual water-use rights, not just visual frontage. Title work and water-rights review are non-negotiable in due diligence.

### 3. Property taxes can be reduced with the right designation
**Source-backed point:** Texas offers wildlife and agricultural valuation exemptions that significantly reduce property tax on qualifying rural land.

**Goal implication:** Carrying-cost projections must include or exclude these exemptions transparently, not optimistic-default to either. The exemption requires qualifying use; "no livestock" constrains options here.

### 4. Conservation easements can lower carry and preserve land character
**Source-backed point:** USDA NRCS and Texas Land Trust Council document programs that pay landowners or reduce taxes in exchange for conservation commitments.

**Goal implication:** If the retreat character (trees, river, wildlife) is the value, conservation easements may be additive to the financial picture. Worth structured evaluation, not first-pass dismissal.

### 5. Short-term-rental viability depends on local rules and demand, not aspirations
**Source-backed point:** Many Hill Country municipalities and HOAs restrict STR. AirDNA-style market data shows occupancy and ADR by submarket.

**Goal implication:** If Airbnb is part of the path, the property must be in a jurisdiction that permits STR and a submarket with demand. Don't bake STR income into the underwriting unless it survives this filter.

## Operational subdomains

### 1. Market scan and listing pipeline
What to watch:
- New listings matching constraints (county, acres, frontage, price)
- Days-on-market for comparable sold listings
- Price-per-acre trend in target counties (Kerr, Comal, Bandera, Kendall, Real)
- Off-market opportunities (broker relationships, drive-around)

### 2. Capital readiness
What to watch:
- Liquid down-payment fund
- Pre-approval status with a rural-friendly lender
- Total debt-service capacity check
- Reserve fund for closing + first-year carry

### 3. Decision criteria
What to watch:
- Hard pass conditions (flood zone severity, no road access, water rights deficient, deed restrictions incompatible with use)
- Strong-yes signals (frontage, mature trees, build site, road frontage, drive time from SA, sun exposure)
- Trade-off matrix (river quality vs. acreage vs. drive time vs. price)

### 4. Due-diligence playbook
What to watch:
- Title insurance and survey
- Water-rights review
- Septic / well evaluation
- Floodplain analysis
- Mineral rights
- Easements / deed restrictions
- Property-tax history and exemption status

### 5. Build / improvement plan (post-acquisition)
What to watch:
- Existing structures' condition vs. teardown
- New build cost and timeline
- Septic / water / power infrastructure
- Permit posture in the county

## How Hermes should use this file

When evaluating river-retreat work, ask in order:
1. Does this advance capital readiness, market knowledge, decision criteria, or deal pipeline? If none, deprioritize.
2. Is this a research task (cheap, parallel-friendly) or a transaction step (sequential, requires human)?
3. Are the hard constraints (county, acres, budget, frontage, no-livestock, no-STR-required) being respected?
4. Is this evidence-collection or commitment? Default to evidence-collection until criteria are clear.
5. Could this surface as a low-stakes auto-execute task (listings scan, county research, lender comparison) or does it need human judgment (visits, offers, negotiations)?

If the answer is "none of the four advancers," the proposed work is probably premature.
