## What the sources directly support

### 1. Brand follows competence, not the other way around
**Source-backed point:** Cal Newport's career-capital thesis: rare and valuable skills create career options; performative branding without underlying competence collapses on contact with reality.

**Fernando implication:** The brand is real. The work is real. Lead with the work, then the framing. Don't manufacture a polish that isn't backed by output.

### 2. Specificity beats breadth
**Source-backed point:** Godin's smallest-viable-audience and Kelly's 1,000-true-fans both argue for narrow, deep, real connection over broad reach.

**Fernando implication:** "Diagnostic automation engineer who came from the wrench" is a real, narrow position. "Generic AI engineer with portfolio" is not. The narrow positioning is the moat.

### 3. Cadence beats intensity
**Source-backed point:** Paul Graham's essays show that consistent thoughtful output over years produces compounding distribution. Sporadic intensity does not.

**Fernando implication:** A monthly long-form post with real substance beats five rushed weekly posts. The content has to survive being read by a senior engineer or hiring manager who already knows the field.

### 4. Discoverability is a one-time setup with maintenance, not a campaign
**Source-backed point:** Google Search Central's basics: clean URLs, proper headings, descriptive meta, real backlinks from real sources, mobile-friendly.

**Fernando implication:** The portfolio site needs the technical hygiene (titles, meta, structured headings). Once set, maintenance is light. This is engineering work, not marketing work.

### 5. The story is the differentiator
**Source-backed point:** Naval and Newport both note that the unique combination of skills + lived story creates positioning that cannot be commoditized.

**Fernando implication:** Mechanic to staff engineer + 11-year arc + diagnostic-automation + BBQ champion + marathon discipline + 2 daughters and engineering-the-life is the story. None of it is fabricated. All of it is positioning.

## Operational subdomains

### 1. Portfolio site (primary anchor)
What it is: https://fernando-fernandez3.github.io/fernando-portfolio/
What to watch:
- Content freshness (last meaningful update)
- Page speed and mobile usability
- Clear CTA (what should a visitor do next: read, contact, follow?)
- Project case studies that show real work

### 2. Long-form writing
What it is: technical and reflective posts on the diagnostic-to-software arc, agent platform building, lived BBQ/marathon/family discipline.
What to watch:
- Posts published per quarter
- Reads / shares / replies (signal, not vanity)
- Inbound conversations triggered (advisory, hiring, collaboration)

### 3. Social presence
What it is: minimal, signal-only feed. Twitter/X, LinkedIn, possibly a personal newsletter.
What to watch:
- Follower growth from real practitioners (not bots / generic followers)
- Reply quality
- Conversion of social to portfolio traffic

### 4. External validation surface
What it is: speaking opportunities, podcast appearances, conference talks, guest posts, third-party features.
What to watch:
- Inbound speaking / podcast invitations per quarter
- Mentions in third-party sources (real ones, not link farms)

### 5. Network compounding
What it is: the people Fernando talks to as a result of the brand existing.
What to watch:
- New genuine connections per month
- Recurring conversations with senior practitioners
- Collaboration opportunities (advisory, side work, joint projects)

## What is still inference, not source fact

- That the mechanic-to-staff-engineer arc is the strongest narrative hook (it might be the diagnostic-automation impact metrics instead, or the agentic-platform work)
- That long-form writing is the right primary medium (could be video, given the BBQ and marathon visual material)
- That LinkedIn vs. Twitter/X vs. personal blog ordering matters less than content quality (could be wrong if one platform's audience converts disproportionately)
- That a monthly post cadence is the right rhythm (could be quarterly long-form + biweekly notes)

## How Hermes should use this file

When evaluating personal-brand work, ask in order:
1. Does this reflect real work, or is it polish without substance?
2. Does it sharpen the narrow positioning or dilute it toward generic?
3. Is the cadence steady (favored) or spiky (de-prioritize)?
4. Will this generate inbound conversations from real practitioners?
5. Is this a one-time setup with maintenance, or a perpetual marketing campaign? Prefer the former.

If the answer leans toward polish-without-substance, broad-not-narrow, or perpetual-campaign, the proposed work is probably the wrong shape.
